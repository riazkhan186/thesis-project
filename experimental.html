4.1 Introduction

Most developing countries are experiencing rapid population growth due to high fertility rates. While high birth rates, reduced mortality rates, and the use of lowexpansion and contraception are some of the reasons for the rapid population growth in these countries, contraception is a way to limit pregnancy and place birth in order to achieve a desired family size. Contraception helps improve women's reproductive health thereby reducing maternal and child morbidity. Extensive population data and socioeconomic factors influence the current use of contraceptives through proximal factors such as spatial communication, women's sexual empowerment, access to services and attitudes, and knowledge of family planning. Reproduction is seen as the sole responsibility of women in most parts of the world, although this dual commitment is often forgotten by men by health clients, especially when it comes to family planning services. The role of women in family planning has been gaining more attention recently as population planners around the world have begun to recognize the importance of male influence in reproductive decision making. Men have a direct and major role in contraceptive decision making, but they also have an indirect role as an influential factor in women's lives regarding their own economic, social and family needs. In the above situation, proper research is needed in family planning, because we can only control the situation by proper checks for population growth. [2].

4.4 Summary

The inquiries provide representative information on knowledge, attitudes, practices, reasons for motivating women's participation in FP, and barriers to the use of FP by adult women living in rural areas. In developing countries, the lack of family planning services for women who want to prevent or postpone future pregnancies inevitably leads to increased maternal mortality, horror rates, and both social and economic costs. Valuable minerals and resources needed to keep. These types of stores of minerals and resources are also criticized for allowing women to endure the process related to delivery. In a country like Bangladesh, high maternal mortality rate (MMR) and infant mortality rate (IMR) consider on the one hand why more family planning services are not available and if they are available then why people do not use them. Knowledge is a key factor in influencing attitudes toward use and family planning practices. If people are well informed about the benefits, methods used and their side effects, they can have a positive attitude and the majority will take part. Studies have shown that in many cases women of reproductive age are generally informed about family planning, but do not have a detailed knowledge of their activities and methods of use. One of the reasons for women's patronizing knowledge about contraception lies in their access to information: mainly from the media and informally from relatives and friends. Radios and televisions are often a source of information for women. This was reflected in the current study with slight differences.
