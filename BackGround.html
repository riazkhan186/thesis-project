2.1 Introduction

Family planning programs often do not provide reproductive and sexual health care. Women of all ages, especially teenage girls, are not present. Reproductive health experts have expressed concern about the government's program to abandon brides and unmarried but sexually active adolescents, according to a New Age report on Sunday. The marriage rate for adolescents is one percent, and the rate of contraceptive use among adolescents is 4 percent. The unreasonable need for contraceptives is even greater among adolescents. Experts blame the poor quality of service at union-level family planning centers and say field workers are not going door-to-door to inform couples about reproductive health and contraceptive preferences. It is the responsibility of the family planning department to manage the centers effectively, but there is a lack of measures to identify failures. Experts also point to the increasing number of sexually active adolescents. The issue is reflected in the level of silence on the level of social bargaining that excludes this category from any reproductive health care. As public centers limit their service to married couples, unsafe and confidential abortions increase in this group. Programs need to be carefully created to allow policy makers to participate in health concerns rather than explicitly approving social silence on the culturally sensitive issue of adolescent sexual health. This silence and limited focus on adult couples is problematic because recently Bangladesh has amended the law to allow a certain amount of child marriage. Many public health workers have been asking the government for decades to convert family planning programs into a reproductive healthcare program. Union health centers are contraceptive choices only when women of all ages suffer from various health problems. There is no place for women to get health information, and the high number of genital fistulas is an example of this. Gynecologists have previously said that women with the disease often feel ashamed to see a physician and are not well aware of the free treatment available. Education and awareness of sexual health should therefore be an integral part of these national health centers. Public health policy makers must leave this declining view of women's health and expand services for wider sexual and reproductive health. They also need to consider sexual health education for adolescents so that they can make informed choices and become aware of reproductive health concerns. Policy-level silence on sexual health will only increase the risk of unsafe abortion, unwanted pregnancy and other health complications.

One of the biggest strategies to fund this project is focusing on young people as clients in need of this service. Also, contraceptives are funded by most donors and the government should plan for their financial protection in the long run.
